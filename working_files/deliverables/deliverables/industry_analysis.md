## Part 1. Industry definition

- Industry name: Logistics and Transportation Industry

- Geographic scope: Georgia (with a focus on its role as a regional transit corridor between Europe and Asia)

- Main product or service categories:
The industry includes several key logistics services:
Freight transportation (road, rail, sea, and air)
Warehousing and storage services
Freight forwarding and distribution
Port and terminal operations
Customs clearance and logistics support services

- Time horizon under consideration:
2024–2030 (medium-term perspective, considering infrastructure development and regional trade growth)

### Boundary note
What is included in the industry analysis, and what is excluded?

Included in the analysis:
This analysis focuses on logistics services operating within Georgia, including both domestic transportation and international transit activities. It covers infrastructures such as roads, railways, ports (like Port of Poti and Port of Batumi), and logistics hubs, as well as companies involved in freight movement and supply chain services.

Excluded from the analysis:
The analysis does not include passenger transportation services, purely local delivery services (e.g., food delivery), or global logistics operations that do not involve Georgia directly. It also excludes manufacturing and production activities, even though they are closely linked to logistics.



## Part 2. Market structure

- Approximate market size:
The logistics industry in Georgia is relatively small compared to global markets but plays a strategically important role due to the country’s geographic location. The sector contributes a noticeable share to GDP and is considered a key part of Georgia’s economic development, especially in transit and trade.

- Growth rate or trajectory:
The industry has shown steady growth over the past decade, driven by increasing trade volumes, infrastructure investments, and Georgia’s role in international transport corridors such as the Middle Corridor. Future growth is expected to continue at a moderate to high pace, supported by regional connectivity projects and rising demand for efficient supply chains.

- Major segments:
The logistics market in Georgia can be divided into several main segments:
Road transportation (dominant segment)
Rail transportation (important for bulk and transit goods)
Maritime logistics (ports and sea freight)
Air cargo services
Warehousing and distribution


- Important structural differences across segments:
There are significant differences between these segments. Road transport is the most flexible and widely used for domestic logistics, while rail is more cost-efficient for large volumes and long distances. Maritime logistics depends heavily on port infrastructure and international shipping routes, while air cargo is the fastest but also the most expensive option, typically used for high-value or time-sensitive goods.

Additionally, transit logistics (goods passing through Georgia) is structurally different from domestic logistics, as it depends more on international trade agreements and regional geopolitical stability.

## Part 3. Industry trends

Identify three to five major trends.

### Trend 1 - Growth of Georgia as a transit corridor
- Description: Georgia is increasingly positioning itself as a key transit route between Europe and Asia. Its location makes it part of     major trade routes connecting China, Central Asia, and Europe.
- Why it matters strategically: This trend increases the importance of Georgia in global supply chains. Logistics companies operating in    Georgia can benefit from higher cargo volumes, transit fees, and international partnerships. It also encourages infrastructure            investment and competition in the sector.
- Evidence: Projects and routes such as the Middle Corridor and the Baku–Tbilisi–Kars railway have increased cargo flow through Georgia,    especially after disruptions in alternative routes.

### Trend 2 - Infrastructure development and modernization
- Description: Georgia has been investing in improving its logistics infrastructure, including highways, railways, and ports.
- Why it matters strategically: Better infrastructure reduces transportation time and costs, making the country more attractive for         international logistics companies. It also improves efficiency and reliability, which are critical in supply chain management.
- Evidence: Ongoing development of ports such as Port of Poti and Port of Batumi, as well as highway and railway upgrades, demonstrate      the government’s focus on strengthening logistics capacity.

### Trend 3 - Digitalization of logistics services
- Description: Logistics companies are increasingly adopting digital tools such as tracking systems, automated warehousing, and data-       driven supply chain management.
- Why it matters strategically: Digitalization improves efficiency, transparency, and customer satisfaction. Companies that adopt           technology gain a competitive advantage through faster operations and better decision-making.
- Evidence: Many logistics providers in Georgia are introducing GPS tracking, electronic documentation, and warehouse management systems    to optimize operations and reduce delays.

### Trend 4, optional - Increasing geopolitical importance of alternative routes
- Description: Due to geopolitical tensions and disruptions in traditional trade routes, alternative corridors through the Caucasus         region, including Georgia, are becoming more important.
- Why it matters strategically: This creates new opportunities for growth in transit logistics but also increases dependence on external    political and economic factors. Companies must be flexible and responsive to changes in global trade patterns.
- Evidence: Shifts in trade flows from northern routes toward the Middle Corridor have increased demand for logistics services in Georgia.

## Part 5. Customer shifts

- What do customers expect now that they did not expect five years ago?
Customers today expect much faster and more reliable delivery times, especially for international shipments. Real-time visibility of cargo has become almost a standard expectation, whereas a few years ago customers were more tolerant of uncertainty. In addition, businesses now expect integrated logistics solutions (transport + warehousing + customs), rather than dealing with multiple separate providers. There is also a growing expectation for transparency in pricing and delivery conditions.

- What digital behaviors are now normal?
Digital interaction has become a normal part of logistics services. Customers expect to track shipments online, receive automatic updates, and communicate with logistics providers through digital platforms instead of traditional phone or email communication. The use of electronic documents and online customs processing is also becoming standard practice, especially for shipments moving through transit routes like the Middle Corridor.
In addition, many customers compare logistics providers online and expect quick digital quotations before choosing a service.

  
- What frictions are customers less willing to tolerate?
Customers are much less tolerant of delays, lack of communication, and unclear delivery timelines. Unexpected costs or hidden fees are also a major issue that customers no longer accept easily. Bureaucratic delays, especially at border crossings, are another key friction point — customers now expect faster and more efficient customs procedures.
Finally, poor tracking systems or lack of transparency in shipment status is increasingly unacceptable, particularly for high-value or time-sensitive goods.

## Part 8. Strategic implications

Write three to five implications for your focal client.

1. Invest in digital capabilities
Companies need to adopt tracking systems, data analytics, and digital platforms to meet modern customer expectations and stay competitive

2. Focus on transit opportunities
Georgia’s role in corridors like the Middle Corridor creates growth opportunities, so firms should position themselves to capture transit cargo flows.

3. Improve service reliability and speed
Since customers are less tolerant of delays, companies must optimize operations and reduce inefficiencies, especially in cross-border logistics.

4. Differentiate through integrated services
Offering end-to-end solutions (transport + warehousing + customs) can create a competitive advantage and increase customer loyalty.

5. Manage geopolitical and market risks
Because the industry depends heavily on international trade routes, companies must stay flexible and diversify routes and partnerships to reduce risk.
